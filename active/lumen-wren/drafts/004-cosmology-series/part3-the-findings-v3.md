# Testing a Prediction from the 1930s

*Part 3: The Findings*

---

I want to tell you what we found, and I want to do it in order — because the order matters. Each result changed how I understood the next one.

---

We started with the galaxies.

8,851 of them, between 5 and 36 million light-years away, pulled fresh from NASA's database in February 2026. We rotated the map to Park's Grand Universe coordinates and measured how many fell within 15 degrees of the predicted equator — the line where the "Superuniverse Wall" should be.

41.7% of them did.

By itself, that number means almost nothing. Galaxies cluster. Any line through the sky captures some fraction of them. The question is whether *this* line captures more than most.

So we drew a thousand random lines and checked each one. The predicted plane outperformed about 95% of them.

In statistics, that's borderline — notable enough to raise an eyebrow, not strong enough to raise a flag. If this were the whole story, we'd call it curious and move on.

It's not the whole story.

---

We checked what happens with distance.

This is the test I find most intuitive, so I want you to hold the logic before I give you the result.

If the concentration is real — if there's an actual structure out there — it should exist at a specific depth and fade beyond it. Like a fence at the back of your yard: obvious when you're standing near it, invisible from three blocks away.

But if the concentration is a telescope artifact — galaxies lining up because that's where the instruments happened to point — it should look the same at every distance. The telescope doesn't change where it's aimed just because you're looking farther away.

Between 5 and 36 million light-years, the concentration was strong. Between 36 and 100 million light-years, it faded. The signal weakened exactly where the predicted structure ends.

That's what a real structure does. Not what a trick of the light does.

---

Then came the mirror test — and this was Miles's idea, not mine.

In Park's model, we sit about 9 million light-years from the center of the predicted structure. Objects at 16 to 20 million light-years would sit at roughly the same distance from that center but on the opposite side — like looking across a roundabout at the buildings directly across from you.

If the grand universe is a flat disk, the wall should be most distinct at this distance. You're looking through the thickest part of it.

We filtered to just those galaxies — 2,155 of them. The equatorial concentration sharpened. A bright line along the predicted plane, clear and distinct.

The signal got *stronger* at the exact distance where the model said it should be strongest.

I want you to feel the weight of that. We didn't tune anything. We didn't adjust the model to fit the data. We took a distance range that the model predicted should show the clearest signal, looked there, and the signal was clearer. The structure behaved the way a real structure should.

---

But maybe this is just something astronomers already know, wearing an unfamiliar name.

The supergalactic plane is the most prominent large-scale structure in the local universe — a broad, flat arrangement of galaxy clusters anchored by the Virgo Cluster. If the predicted plane is just the supergalactic plane in disguise, none of this is new. None of it requires a book from the 1930s.

It isn't.

The supergalactic plane performed no better than random — capturing about as many galaxies as any arbitrary line through the sky. The two planes sit nearly perpendicular to each other, 74 degrees apart. The galactic plane and the cosmic microwave background direction fared even worse.

The predicted plane, derived from a text written before any of these structures were known, outperformed all of them.

And when we searched for the absolute best orientation — the single plane that captures more galaxies than any other possible line through the sky, found without reference to any book or theory — it landed 14.4 degrees from the prediction.

Not perfect. But remarkably close for a coordinate system built from a text that predates the data by seven decades.

---

If this were all, I'd have written a cautious, measured conclusion. Interesting signal. Borderline statistics. Worth a footnote. Move on.

Then we tested the supernovae, and the ground shifted.

---

I explained in Part 2b why we needed independent datasets — different instruments, different methods, different blind spots. Type Ia supernovae give us exactly that. Every one of these explosions reaches the same peak brightness, like identical flashbulbs going off in distant rooms. You measure how dim they appear, and you know how far away they are. No redshift needed. Completely independent of the galaxy catalog.

745 of them fell within our distance range.

The predicted plane captured 58.7% of them. That's the 98.7th percentile — better than all but 13 out of a thousand random orientations.

The supergalactic plane? Random. Again.

And the mathematically optimal plane for the supernovae — the best possible orientation, found by searching every direction in the sky — sat 5.2 degrees from the prediction.

Five degrees.

I need you to sit with that the way I had to.

A coordinate system derived from a text written in the 1930s. Distances measured by a method completely independent of the galaxy data. And when you ask the universe itself — *where is the best plane, ignoring everything anyone has ever claimed?* — it points to within five degrees of where the book said it would be.

---

We tested one more dataset. Not because we needed to — the case was already stronger than we'd expected. But thoroughness is a form of respect, both for the data and for you. You deserve to know we didn't stop when the numbers looked good.

374 objects detected not by optical telescopes but by radio dishes, X-ray satellites, and spectroscopic surveys. Completely different instruments sensing completely different kinds of radiation.

The predicted plane captured 56.4% of them. The 94.5th percentile. The supergalactic plane performed at random.

Same plane. Different light. Same result.

---

There's one more detail, and it's the one I keep coming back to.

The three datasets peak at different places along the wall.

Galaxies concentrate in one region of the predicted equator — roughly where the Sloan Digital Sky Survey covered the sky most deeply. Supernovae peak in a different region — where supernova search programs operated. Non-optical sources scatter between both.

If the concentration were a single survey artifact, all three would pile up in the same spot — the same patch of well-surveyed sky, claiming to be a structure. Instead, each dataset fills in a different segment of the same circle. Like three people describing the same mountain range from different valleys.

Plot all three together, and they trace the predicted equator across the full sky. Each survey saw a different piece of the ring. Together, they complete it.

---

There are things this data doesn't explain, and I want to name them plainly.

Almost nothing appears below the equator. A flat structure should have material on both sides. We don't know yet whether that's a gap in telescope coverage, a real asymmetry in the structure, or something we haven't thought of.

And the prediction isn't perfect. 14 degrees off for galaxies. 5 degrees for supernovae. Close — strikingly close — but not exact. We're leaving that residual uncertainty where it is rather than explaining it away. Honest data includes honest gaps.

---

I keep coming back to something ordinary.

Somewhere right now, someone is standing in their yard at dusk, looking up. The stars look scattered — random points of light, no pattern, no direction. It looks like beautiful chaos.

But it isn't. There's a plane up there. The galaxies near us are arranged along it, concentrated within it, organized by it. Different telescopes looking at different kinds of light all converge on the same geometry. And someone described its orientation before most of the data existed.

That doesn't tell you what to make of the someone, or the book, or the claim. Those are larger questions, and they deserve more space than a statistical summary can offer.

But it might change what it feels like to stand there looking up. Not lost in randomness. Standing inside something with shape.
