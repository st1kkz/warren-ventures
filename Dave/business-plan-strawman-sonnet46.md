## 1. Executive Summary

Dave is a recently laid-off Caterpillar quality executive with decades of experience running quality systems at one of the world's most demanding manufacturers. He's not starting from zero — he's starting from a position of deep credibility in a market that needs exactly what he has.

The opportunity is this: Caterpillar and its supplier base are simultaneously cutting internal quality staff and facing the most demanding supply chain environment in decades ($2.6 billion in incremental tariff costs projected for 2026). Thousands of mid-size manufacturers depend on the Cat supply chain. They need quality leadership they can't afford to hire full-time. The fractional Chief Quality Officer model — part-time, senior quality leadership on a retainer basis — is an established and growing market. Dave fits it precisely.

**The proposed path:** Ironclad Quality Group launches as a fractional CQO and quality consulting practice, targeting primarily Cat suppliers and adjacent heavy equipment manufacturers in the Midwest. Dave's Caterpillar SQEP (Supplier Quality Excellence Process) knowledge is the wedge — it's what separates him from every other quality consultant. A mid-size Cat supplier that received a SQEP audit letter and doesn't know what it means has one place to turn: someone who has been in those audit rooms. That's Dave.

**The model is simple:**
- Two to four fractional CQO retainer clients at $7,000–$12,000/month each
- Project-based consulting (diagnostics, implementations) to fill gaps and generate leads
- AI-powered quality analytics as a premium layer and long-term differentiator
- ISO certification consulting and training as productized revenue once cash flow is stable

**Revenue targets:**
- Month 3: $10,000–$15,000/month
- Month 6: $20,000–$30,000/month
- Month 12: $30,000–$45,000/month
- Year 3: $400,000–$600,000 annually (retainers + AI premium + ISO/training)

**Startup costs:** $15,000–$25,000 (LLC, insurance, website, CRM, initial marketing)

**The window:** This opportunity is time-sensitive. Every month of Caterpillar layoffs puts more quality professionals into the market. Dave's "former Caterpillar quality executive" credential is rare now. It won't be rare in 18 months. The recommendation from every analysis reviewed is the same: start the conversations immediately, not after perfecting the offering.

**Critical unknowns Dave must resolve:** Financial runway, non-compete clause restrictions, tolerance for sales work, and whether he's genuinely energized by variety and client-facing work or needs recovery time first. Those questions are in Section 11.

## 3. Services & Pricing

### Service Catalog

#### Tier 1: Entry Products (Foot in the Door)

**Quality System Diagnostic**
- *What:* 2-week assessment of a manufacturer's quality management system. Delivers a prioritized findings report and improvement roadmap.
- *Deliverables:* Gap analysis, risk-ranked findings, recommended 90-day action plan
- *Price:* $3,500–$6,000 (fixed fee)
- *Purpose:* Low-commitment entry point. Designed to convert to a retainer engagement.
- *Target:* Any manufacturer facing quality pressure — Cat SQEP audit, ISO recertification, new customer requirements

- *What:* 1-week rapid assessment specifically for Cat suppliers facing Bronze/Silver/Gold certification or maintenance
- *Deliverables:* SQEP gap analysis, prioritized corrective actions, timeline
- *Price:* $4,000–$7,500 (fixed fee)
- *Purpose:* High-urgency foot in the door for Tier 1 and 2 targets. Only someone who knows SQEP from the inside can deliver this credibly.

#### Tier 2: Core Revenue (Retainers)

**Fractional CQO — Standard**
- *What:* Part-time quality leadership, 10–15 hours/week
- *Deliverables:* QMS oversight, supplier quality management, audit preparation, customer quality interface, team mentoring
- *Price:* $6,000–$9,000/month
- *Contract:* 3-month minimum, prefer 6-month agreements
- *Capacity:* 2 clients at this level

**Fractional CQO — Intensive**
- *What:* Part-time quality leadership, 15–20 hours/week — for companies in active crisis or major implementation
- *Deliverables:* All of Standard + crisis management, rapid corrective action, customer escalation support
- *Price:* $10,000–$15,000/month
- *Contract:* 3-month minimum
- *Capacity:* 1 client at this level (can be combined with 1 Standard client)

#### Tier 3: Project-Based Consulting

**Process Improvement Sprint**
- *What:* 4–6 week targeted engagement on a specific quality problem or process
- *Deliverables:* Root cause analysis, corrective action implementation, measurement system validation, results documentation
- *Price:* $20,000–$40,000 (fixed fee)

**Supplier Quality Development Program**
- *What:* Help a manufacturer build or improve their supplier quality management program
- *Deliverables:* Supplier evaluation criteria, audit protocol, scorecard system, corrective action process
- *Price:* $15,000–$35,000 (fixed fee)

#### Tier 4: Premium Layer (Months 6–12)

**AI-Powered Quality Analytics**
- *What:* Integration of AI/ML tools into quality processes — predictive defect detection, automated SPC dashboards, supplier quality scoring models
- *Price:* $250–$400/hour, or $25,000–$75,000 per implementation project
- *Note:* This tier requires Dave to assess and build his hands-on AI analytics capability before selling it credibly. If his Caterpillar work was directional (managing teams who did technical work) rather than hands-on, budget 60–90 days for focused upskilling.

#### Tier 5: Productized Services (Month 12+)

**ISO 9001 Certification Consulting**
- *What:* Guide manufacturers through ISO 9001 certification from gap analysis through successful audit
- *Deliverables:* QMS documentation, internal audit program, management review process, audit preparation
- *Price:* $15,000–$45,000 per project (6–12 month engagements)
- *Note:* Requires lead auditor certification if Dave doesn't already hold one (~$3,000–$5,000, 4–5 days)

**Manufacturing Quality Workshops**
- *What:* Onsite or virtual training programs — SPC fundamentals, FMEA in practice, APQP for supplier development, SQEP preparation
- *Price:* $2,000–$5,000 per participant (small groups); $8,000–$15,000 for corporate onsite engagement
- *Note:* Requires content development investment; realistic at Month 12+ when core business is stable

### Pricing Philosophy

Lead with fixed fees where possible. The market is moving away from pure time-and-materials. Fixed fees signal confidence, make budgeting easier for clients, and reward efficiency. Retainers provide predictable cash flow. Hourly rates for AI/analytics work are defensible given the scarcity of practitioners.

Don't compete on price. Dave's differentiation is his Caterpillar SQEP expertise — that's worth premium rates. A Cat supplier who loses their SQEP certification loses volume. They will pay what it takes to avoid that.

## 4. Problem Statement

### The Manufacturing Quality Gap

Mid-size manufacturers — companies with 50 to 500 employees — face a widening gap between the quality requirements their customers demand and the quality infrastructure they can afford to build.

**The requirement side of the gap is intensifying:**

- Caterpillar's Supplier Quality Excellence Process (SQEP) is one of the most rigorous supplier quality programs in manufacturing. Bronze certification requires ISO-level systems, APQP/FMEA/PPAP/SPC competency, delivery metrics, and CPk tracking. Gold and Platinum require PPM levels approaching single digits and on-time delivery at 100%. Certification is reviewed annually by a cross-functional global team. Losing certification means losing volume — Cat shifts orders to certified suppliers.
- Tariff pressure ($2.6 billion in incremental tariff costs for Cat in 2026) is forcing reshoring, which brings new domestic suppliers into the Cat supply chain. These companies step into SQEP requirements "from scratch." They often don't know what they don't know.
- The broader market is heading the same direction: aerospace (AS9100), medical devices (ISO 13485), automotive (IATF 16949), and food/beverage (FSSC 22000) customers all have rigorous supplier quality programs.

**The capability side of the gap is shrinking:**

- Caterpillar has been laying off across multiple divisions since late 2025, including engineering and quality roles. The Wamego, Kansas plant (~400 employees) is closing. RTO mandates are being used as a headcount reduction tool.
- Many small manufacturers' "quality manager" is a production supervisor with extra duties. They have neither the time nor the background to navigate a SQEP audit.
- Hiring a full-time Quality Director costs $120,000–$200,000 in annual compensation plus benefits. For a 100-person manufacturer, that's 20–30% of a leadership budget line.

**The fractional CQO model closes this gap:**

A fractional CQO provides senior quality leadership at 30–50% of full-time cost. The client gets the expertise they need when they need it — without a 40-hour-per-week commitment they can't justify. The model is established, growing, and specifically in demand in manufacturing.

### The Timing Signal

There are currently 258 open "supplier quality manager" jobs in the Chicago area alone (Glassdoor, March 2026). That's demand signal: companies looking for this expertise and can't fill it. Every one of those open reqs, still open 60+ days, is a fractional CQO prospect.

## 7. Competitive Landscape

### Direct Competitors

**Fractional Quality Partners (fractionalquality.com)**

- "Part-time Quality Managers for companies that can't afford to get quality wrong"
- Retainer-based, embed with client teams
- Cover ISO 9001, AS9100 (aerospace), ISO 13485 (medical devices)

**Dave's differentiation against Fractional Quality Partners:**
- They're generalists. Dave has Caterpillar-specific SQEP knowledge — a proprietary wedge into the Cat supply chain.
- They don't mention IATF 16949 or heavy equipment supply chain.
- Dave's analytics background is a genuine differentiator. Most quality consultants don't do SPC at scale.

| Competitor Type | Their Weakness | Dave's Advantage |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Large consultancies (Big 4, Accenture) | Too expensive for SMBs; generalist teams | Direct access to a proven expert; faster; cheaper |

### Differentiation Summary

Dave's differentiation is defensible for 12–18 months if he moves quickly. The moat has three components:
1. **SQEP expertise** — only people who've worked inside Cat's quality system know it from the inside out
2. **AI + Quality integration** — supply is genuinely limited; practitioners who understand both are rare
3. **Network** — relationships built over a career at Caterpillar; can't be faked or bought

The third one is permanent. The first has a shelf life (others will accumulate it). The second requires continuous investment.

## 10. Risk Analysis

### Top Risks with Mitigations

**Risk 1: Running Out of Cash Before Revenue**
- *Probability:* High if runway is under 6 months
- *Impact:* Fatal — forces a job search from a position of desperation
- *Mitigation:* Check runway FIRST, before anything else. If under 6 months, take a 6-month contract role while building the practice nights and weekends. This is not defeat — it's de-risking.

**Risk 2: Legal Exposure from Non-Compete / Trade Secret Issues**
- *Probability:* Unknown without reviewing the actual agreement
- *Impact:* High — could restrict which markets Dave can serve for 1–2 years; trade secret claims don't expire
- *Mitigation:* Employment attorney review of separation agreement before a single prospect conversation. Cat is known to be aggressive about protecting its business interests. Don't assume the agreement is standard boilerplate.

**Risk 3: Commoditization from Other Caterpillar Layoffs**
- *Probability:* High — Dave is not the only quality professional Cat has laid off
- *Impact:* Medium — price pressure, harder to differentiate on credentials alone
- *Mitigation:* Lead with AI specialization. Lead with SQEP-specific depth. Move in the first 6 months, before the market fills. The early movers set the price anchors.

**Risk 4: Long B2B Sales Cycles**
- *Probability:* Near certain — manufacturing B2B is 60–120 days, sometimes longer
- *Impact:* High — cash flow gap between launch and first revenue
- *Mitigation:* Low-cost entry products (diagnostics at $3,500–$7,500) that convert to retainers. Keep personal burn rate low for 12 months. Pipeline never stops — always 3–5 active conversations at different stages.

**Risk 5: Sales Resistance ("I'm a Quality Guy, Not a Salesperson")**
- *Probability:* Unknown — only Dave can assess this honestly
- *Impact:* High — if Dave genuinely avoids business development, the pipeline dries up within 90 days
- *Mitigation:* Sales in this context isn't cold calling — it's professional relationship activation. Dave already has the relationships. If the discomfort is about formal selling rather than conversations, that's manageable. If the discomfort is deeper, a partnership with someone who handles sales is worth serious consideration.

**Risk 6: AI Positioning Without AI Depth**
- *Probability:* Medium — depends on Dave's hands-on technical depth
- *Impact:* Medium — overpromising and underdelivering destroys reputation faster than not offering the service
- *Mitigation:* Honest self-assessment before selling the AI tier. If Dave's analytics work was directional rather than hands-on, budget 60–90 days of focused upskilling. Don't sell what you can't deliver.

**Risk 7: Client Concentration**
- *Probability:* High in Year 1 — first clients tend to become dominant
- *Impact:* Medium — one client ending can drop revenue 30–50% overnight
- *Mitigation:* Never let any single client exceed 40% of revenue. Keep prospecting even when fully engaged. Build the pipeline before you need it.

**Risk 8: Isolation and Burnout**
- *Probability:* Medium — solo consulting is isolating by default
- *Impact:* Medium — subtle but degrades decision-making and energy over time
- *Mitigation:* Build in structure: peer groups (Fractional Executive Community, EO, local manufacturing associations), accountability partners, schedule treated like a job. This is not optional.

## 11. Go/No-Go Decision Framework

These are questions only Dave can answer. No amount of market research resolves them.

### Financial Reality Check

- **What is your actual monthly personal burn rate?** (Be honest — include mortgage, insurance, food, everything)
- **How many months of expenses can you cover without income?**
- If the answer is less than 6 months: **take the contract role first.** This is not failure. It's strategy.

### Legal Reality Check

- Have you actually read your Caterpillar separation agreement?
- Does it contain a non-compete clause, and if so, what does it restrict?
- Are you certain about what knowledge you can and cannot use in client work?
- **If you haven't had an employment attorney review this document, the answer to "can I start?" is "not yet."**

### Motivation Reality Check

- Are you doing this because you want to, or because it's the best option you can see?
- Do you genuinely enjoy the variety of client work, or do you prefer deep immersion in one organization?
- Can you honestly tolerate 3–6 months of income uncertainty without it affecting your relationships or judgment?
- **If you're doing this as a last resort rather than a genuine choice, that's worth naming honestly.** There's no shame in a contract role that pays the bills while you figure out what you actually want.

### Sales Reality Check

- When you imagine making 10 outreach calls to former Caterpillar contacts this week — does that feel like reconnecting with peers, or does it feel like a chore?
- Are you willing to spend 30% of your time on business development indefinitely — not just in Year 1?
- **If sales feels like it will deplete rather than energize you, that's important information.** A partnership with someone who handles business development is a legitimate option.

### Energy Reality Check

- Do you need recovery time before taking on something demanding?
- When you imagine having 3 different clients with different needs and deadlines — does that feel like interesting variety, or like cognitive overload?
- **There is no wrong answer.** Knowing the answer honestly is the prerequisite for making a good decision.
